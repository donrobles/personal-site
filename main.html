1. Introduction

I sat in front of my computer for roughly an hour, trying to write my introduction, until I decided to just start typing and let the thoughts flow.

My name is Don Robles, and I already know you’re trying to learn more about me! Well, you’ve come to the right place! I don’t take many things seriously (mainly myself), except for serious matters (like myself), and hopefully this site reflects that! Maybe. I’m conflicted. I want to focus solely on my skills, portraying myself as a professional who accomplishes everything he sets his mind to. However, I also just want to finish this site because it’s been on the to-do list for several years!

In the end, both those trains of thought convey myself because, for better or worse, I’m a conflicted person, and without one of them, I wouldn’t be me. This is because my two great passions are building things, and solving problems, and it's these two joys that are at the base of everything I deem worthwhile undertaking.

These joys guide me in what I do given the nature of their interaction within myself.

I want to solves problems like a coder, splitting them into bite-sized pieces, addressing each of them until the full solution is implemented, while my desire to build and create influences how the problem is solved. I’ve found myself pulling from past experiences to build new and unique solutions to serve me again in the future!

In layman’s terms, I’m a coder, and an artist. A professional who enjoys being silly. Or as I like to put it, I’m a focused individual with a carefree personality. For a time I thought of dropping one part of myself to focus solely on the other, but I’ve since learned they’re both intrinsically linked to who I am, and I’m destined to continue struggling to satiate both of them.

Honestly, it’s not hard, and it keeps life fresh for me. One day I’ll be in a bout of solving life’s greatest problems, while another I’m furiously scribbling away in my sketchbook. Maybe one day I’ll truly let one go (I doubt it), but until then, I know I’ll always have something to keep my days occupied!

Thanks for reading, I hope you enjoy what you've learn about me! (I promise, I’m cooler in person!)

Don Robles

PS: Do you know “PS” stands for “Post Script”? There’s your fun fact of the day!

3. Accomplishments

I've learned recently, since about 2014, I must always have a goal to work towards, otherwise I truly wont feel fulfilled with my time.

Following are some of my favorite accomplishments in my life thus far!

Authentic Athens Marathon

Have you ever ran a marathon? No? Good, because physically and mentally it taxed me more than anything before it (but run one if you're committed). The grueling experience taught me a new level of determination I had for accomplishing my goals, as it required every ounce of discipline (and then some) to keep my feet moving for what felt like an eternity!

Thanks to the difficulty, my newfound understanding of my mental fortitude has allowed me to look on my problems since then and say, “This isn’t nearly as exhausting or hard, I can handle this.” And continue operating at a mental level I know I can sustain.

When working out, I say REALLY mean things to myself, knowing the pain, soreness, and exhaustion is nothing in comparison, and I push myself harder. I doubt I’d run another marathon (maybe), but it was totally worth the effort and training required of me to complete it!

And in case you’re wondering, I ran the Authentic Marathon in Greece on 11-12-16 with a time of 5:05:48.

An Ode To Dedication

In 2015, I was dispirited with my creative state-of-mind, and set out to rectify that. Prior to this project, I attempted a "200 Drawings In A Year" project, but failed at it because of the insurmountable creative barrier of running out of interesting ideas. From that experience, I learned I needed to feel connected to my drawings, I couldn't "just draw something", so this project addressed those issues.

At the time, graffiti art was an interesting format to me, so I knew I wanted to draw words. Next, I wrote a 52-word poem about the project, focused on the dedication required to complete it. Each of the 52 words would be drawn each week, for a year, so I’d have 52 drawings of words, making the poem, thus literally conveying the dedication required to complete the project itself.

After setting the foundation, I started the project on April 27th, 2015 and kept doing a drawing a week, for about 4 weeks!! Falling behind was lame, but my determination never wavered, and after some life setbacks, I completed the final drawing on December 31st, 2016! It wasn't intentional to finish on the last day of 2016, but with the year ending, and only 2 drawings remaining at the time, I completely stopped everything to make sure I completed them before the year concluded.

Full Tough Mudder

Doing a Tough Mudder was one of my more spontaneous decisions! It started simply when my friend posted on Facebook asking who wanted to do one? And never wanting to say “No” to life, I thought “Sure, why not?” and that was all my reasoning!

If you don't know, a “Tough Mudder” is a 10 mile obstacle course, with an obstacle at each half-mile, and each obstacle usually requires assistance to complete. Basically, the whole course focuses on using teamwork to get through it. I was on a team of 5 people, 3 of whom I never met before the event! (they were friends of friends)

I trained for 3 months to be in proper condition to complete the 10 mile course, and I’m glad I did because while going through the event, I was slightly surprised at the “ease” of it. Not to say I had no help! I certainly did (some obstacles require it), but the physical toll on my body wasn’t nearly as draining as I was anticipating! (my one friend was worn out at the end!)

I think it was because of my prior long distance training for my marathon, but it didn’t detract from my sense of accomplishment once I completed the event! If you decide to do one, I highly recommend having a team of friends do it with, that’s truly where the joy lies!

Landing an Ollie

Since I was a kid, I’ve admired skateboarding culture. I had a skateboard too, but that was my whole experience! Growing up, I’d watch guys doing tricks, envying how cool they looked (and they still look cool). I watched skateboarding videos, shows, and played skateboarding video games, but it was never more than an interest. Until one day I decided I wanted to land an Ollie.

If you know nothing about skateboarding, every (modern) skateboarder ever, whether it’s a pro, a guy in the park, or a kid in the driveway, each of them HAD to land an Ollie. It’s honestly a rite-of-passage amongst skateboarders because any skateboarder will understand exactly what it means to land an Ollie. So why is the Ollie so significant? Because it’s literally just jumping with your skateboard!

This makes the Ollie the basis for every modern day trick, just getting the board into the air! It sounds simple enough, but the technique involved is not natural, intuitive, or even fully understood, and failure is absolutely involved in learning it! This is because the finesse and timing is different for everyone, thus requiring MANY attempts before landing an Ollie, and you're going to hit the ground a few times in the process!

As a result, I definitely earned a few scars as a testament to the effort, but when I landed it, I let out a LOUD yell of success!! No one was around to see it (to my knowledge), but I still remember it to this day! Since then, I’ve had no desire to go pro (maybe one day), and I still don’t skateboard much, but what I did was enough to satisfy my skateboarder desires. At least for now!

1 Hour Drawings

This project is ongoing since there's no defined "end." Given my current work/life/project balance, large artistic endeavors will be limited, so this how I envision continuing my artistic practice. Instead of focusing on creating a large body of interconnected drawings like in "An Ode To Dedication", the goal is simply spending one hour a week actively drawing.

With these drawings, I could focus on anything, working purely with lines, switching to drawing completely freehand, explore a fleeting thought, or even staring aimlessly and drawing an abstraction. Whatever the choice, I'm drawing for just drawing’s sake.

This gives me a format to keep drawing, but with loose guidelines so I can explore my creative mindset without the burden of meeting an overall goal. Hmm... But now that I think about it, wouldn't that make all the drawings an interconnected series??! Crap.

Getting In Shape

The irony here is how natural of a progression it was for me. In college I always ate two sandwiches for lunch. Occasionally it'd be different, but sandwiches were a staple of my diet. After some time, I had an epiphany when I noticed some slight discomfort with eating two sandwiches. I realized I physically uncomfortable eating two sandwiches, so I began eat only one.

At the time, I had no idea this one change would result in dropping 20 pounds over 9 months!! The hilarious part is I didn’t even notice the weight loss until people started commenting on it. Shortly afterward, my pants stopped fitting properly, spurning me to continue improving my overall health!

Today, as I write this, I can say I'm in the best shape of my life, and I aim to keep it that way as long as possible! :)

4. Hobbies/Interests

I have a lot of interests, so what I'm most interested in will likely change over time, but my time with each of them will certainly be valued for their provided experience.

Drawing

My obsession with drawing started out like anything else, a strong desire to eliminate my boredom from a really boring class in my college Freshman year. My professor droned on about a topic that didn't mentally stimulate me, and in that state, to avoid falling asleep, I began to doodling anything in the classroom.

I started with an outline of the ceiling projector, and it progressed to drawing each connection at the back of the projector. From there, I moved upward, drawing the ceiling mount, then moved further out to get each ceiling tile. I kept at it, capturing my perspective of the projector as best I could, and before I knew it, the class was over.

It was a moment of awakening for me as I thought, “Why not take an art class?” realizing anything that could hold my attention the way drawing did, is at least worth looking into. After that, I took my first art class my Sophomore fall semester, another the following spring, declared my Studio Art major at the end of that year, and never looked back!

Music

I don’t remember exactly when I started to love music, but it’s always been part of my life. My musical preferences have changed with the years, but music itself holds a draw I never tire of feeling. Whether for its beat, the chorus, or simply being catchy to the ear, I still remember feeling joy of hearing my first favorite song.

Over the years, this love matured into understanding a songs meaning, the lyrics telling a story, teaching a lesson, or portraying an experience. It was new, and gave me a new reason for keeping my ears tuned into a song, listening to it intently, instead of merely hearing it.

My biggest musical leap occurred not when I thought about a songs construction, but when I began relating to a songs message. Songs began perfectly explaining my feelings, be it regret, anger, relief, excitement, or ambition. This drove a newfound appreciation of past songs, finding a greater meaning in some as I was now able to truly comprehend the musician's message.

Music has been my escape throughout my life, providing whatever I’m looking for, time and time again, and for that, it has my eternal love! :)

Cloud Computing

My interest in Cloud Computing was sudden, but distinct and immediately enthralling. My role at work was shifted from being a Software Developer, to DevOpts member. I was moved because my team lead was bringing in a new coder, and I volunteered to make space on the team for them (the room was already cramped enough!)

Joining the DevOpts team started off puzzling, as I had to learn a new set of tools on the AWS platform, but with each passing day, I found myself continuously drawn into Cloud Computing services. Whereas before I had to learn how to code buttons, levers, and switches, with AWS the level of abstraction stopped at already predefined buttons, levers, and switches.

With that distinction, I realized what could be done with AWS was (in a way) far smaller than what could be done with code, and it provided me with a base on which I was truly comfortable. It was freedom to create something, minus being bogged down in details of how everything worked. Learning AWS has been a joy, and the experience is only getting better as I put more time into grasping everything it can do!

Shell Commands

Now I know what you’re thinking, “Who DOESN’T love shell commands?!” and I’m right there with you! Shell commands are the greatest. Thing. On. EARTH! You get immediate feedback, the commands are (usually) always the same, and they’re purely functional! What’s not to love about that? Seriously, I fail to understand how everybody doesn’t love using a Shell in their day-to-day life (convenience be damned).

But seriously, the immediate-response-like nature of a Shell gives me an odd rare joy. If I have an automation problem, need to test some basic functionality, or even if I want to be lazy with the setup of my environment, the Shell provides joy to my solution.

If I could, I’d work in a Shell for everything I’d ever need to do! I legitimately mean that. Plus, I feel like you look like a genius when you doing everything from a Shell.

Having said all that, if you don't know what a Shell is in relation to computers/programming, don't worry about it, just know I think they're awesome!

Basketball

I love basketball (any James Harden fans?). The irony here is, as a kid, I had no interest in basketball, or sports for that matter. Initially, sports weren't worth watching when I could play video games (that’s all I did until college), but oh how times have changed! (I also watch League of Legends and Apex Legends on Twitch)

Basketball is awesome! It’s competitive! It’s constant entertainment! Sports are all about who can physically and mentally perform at the absolute highest levels, against others who are doing the same, and still come out on top!

After watching basketball for a few years now, it's easier to push myself harder to achieve my desires. Seeing what players have done, sticking to their passions for years, grinding day in and day out, putting in hour after hour of work, sweat, blood, and tears, I decided I have no excuse for not trying to do the same!

I have no delusions of being a NBA Superstar, but seeing the drive of others keeps me going. That’s why I love basketball.

Daydreaming

So you know when you’re in a coffee shop, maybe to meet a friend, or to get your morning cup of Joe, and while you wait, you look around and see that guy sitting at a window, fingers interlaced beneath his chin, staring aimlessly out the window? That’s me. And my day isn’t complete without a daydream. (you’ll rarely see me like this)

Since I was a child, daydreaming has been natural for me. I’d always stare out the car window on long drives, looking at the clouds, street lights, trees, cows, grass, or even other cars. Anything on the other side of the window was more interesting than what was within 3 feet of me, and it remains the same over 20 years later.

Even as I type this, I find myself constantly looking at this tree in front of me, the apartment building to my left, or the clouds in the sky. They’re just there, a part of the world, but in the background of our daily lives, and by acknowledging their presence, I’m giving them life and meaning.

Anyway, daydreaming, it’s cool to me. Try it out sometime!

Dungeons and Dragons

I have a theory Dungeons and Dragons is going to experience a resurgence (if it hasn’t already) because of it’s inherent social requirements. More people (but not all) have more social connections through online interactions, but we aren’t programmed that way, we need other people in our lives! Coffee Shops

5. History

Houston Start

I was born in Houston, Texas, living with my 3 siblings and parents. Growing up the 2nd youngest, my dad always said I was different from my siblings because “You were always focused. I don’t know what it was, but you could see it.” (I think I know what he means now). I usually played video games most of the day, and riding my bike around the nearby park was a constant joy. I always had a curiosity for new places and things, wanting to see what else the world had.

Sadly, those opportunities were rare in my childhood because of a burden that couldn’t be avoided. Growing up, my father had two jobs, one was a day job, and the other was early in the morning throwing newspapers, but it wasn’t only “his” job, it was the family job.

Throwing newspapers, you get paid by papers delivered, instead of hours worked, and when you have 4 kids who can help, you can deliver a lot of newspapers! That’s not a knock against my parents, it’s just what was needed for our family to live, and I’m thankful for the experience as it made me no stranger to working hard at something I didn’t enjoy. It has served me well, but it wasn’t without it’s drawbacks.

Friendly sleepovers never happened, staying up late playing games was uncommon, or just doing whatever kids do (honest question, what do they do?) And through all my life before college, we only had 3 family vacations longer than 3 days. I never “went out” while growing up (if that was a thing). I couldn’t hang out with friends late into the evening at their place. I couldn't stay late at parties. I couldn't be out late at card shops. I just couldn't do much, although I did get the night off after my high school graduation, so that was something.

In retrospect, I’m thankful for avoiding all the teenage stuff because those night activities don’t interest me even now, though I do feel I missed out on something common for everyone else. Still, making friends was hard when I was younger as I always felt constraints on me to figure out who I was, making it hard to connect with others.

Beloit College

Going to Beloit College was by far one of the best things I’ve ever done with my life, right next deciding I was going to be a good person. There was so much that went into my experience of going here, that to write it all down would take building another site with chapters for each year. It was everything I was looking for in a college, and it’ll always have a tender place in my heart for the memories and friends I made there.

The only reason I found out about Beloit College was because my Calculus teacher from high school told me about it, and she only told me about it because she read about it in a book about “Colleges that change your mind about college”. Me, being the unaware high school student I was, thought, “I’ll apply” without much thought afterward, knowing only that it was in Wisconsin and not in Texas. That was really my whole decision-making process for picking a college, that it was outside of Houston (or Texas).

I had little desire to go where I knew my friends were going because I wanted to see more of the world, to go somewhere truly different, and to finally escape needing to deliver newspapers. I only applied to Houston schools to make sure I got in somewhere. After getting accepted, and seeing their generous financial aid package, I immediately chose Beloit College, blindly go somewhere I had never been, and I literally mean that.

I knew nothing about Beloit College other than what I read about it online, their school website, and the acceptance package I received in the mail. I had received an invitation to visit the campus, but was already visiting another college the same weekend as their retreat, so the first time I saw the campus was the day I arrived before orientation. When I later told people about this, they were amazed I could do something like that given I would be there for four years, but it never really fazed me.

As I said, my only concern was the college being outside Houston, other than that, I knew I could work with my situation wherever I went. I still feel that way too, and I think it has served me well thus far!

Baltimore Jump

Before my decision to move to Baltimore, I lived my life like I was on train, sitting down as I moved from event to event with little effort. I was a child, I went to elementary school, then middle school, followed by high school, and rounding it out with college. Going to Baltimore was the first real instance when I truly grasped the understanding of, “I can do anything I want.” and more than that, “I can do it however I wanted.” It was an amazing feeling.

The whole reason I moved there was because my best friend and I made an agreement our senior year at Beloit that wherever he went to graduate school, I’d move there, and if he didn’t get into graduate school, he’d move to Houston with me. He would eventually get accepted at Johns Hopkins and, being true to my word, I moved out there on October 3rd of 2012.

The crazy thing about moving there was my plan (or rather my lack of one) on how I did it. I had no place lined up. I had no job lined up. I barely had money to fund myself while there. The only thing I had was my best friend, which I think is the only reason I was able to pull off such a (in hindsight) reckless move. I was on foodstamps for the first few months because I had no income. I lived off beans and rice because it was cheap. And I took the first job offer I could get, which was thankfully a tech company.

The timing of getting that job couldn’t have been better either because 1 month after I started, my funds were completely dried up, I had less than 100 dollars in my account, and I had to begin paying my student loans (like many others my age). It was definitely a stressful (and fun) time of my life, but it was a truly unique experience to move there with almost nothing, and working my way up to having a place of my own, with a fridge full of food!

Portland Adventure

My need for adventure was 90% of the reason why I decided to move to Portland, OR. As I said before, I moved to Baltimore as part of my agreement with my best friend, but I had no intention of settling there, so I always had a desire to leave at some point. I choose Portland to move to because I was originally going to transfer to the Portland office of the company I was working for. Sadly, that didn’t pan out because I was laid off 2 months before my intended departure, but since I was already getting out my current lease, and looking at places to live there, I figured, “Why not move to Portland?” and so I did!

My original plan wasn’t too much of a plan. I was intending on doing the same thing I had done when I moved to Baltimore, which was to go there with no job or place lined out, and figure it out as I went, with the key difference this time being I didn’t have a friend to stay with while out there. I must admit, this adventure of mine didn’t pan out quite how I would have like. Suffice it to say, this adventure was a failure.

Not having a job lined up was perhaps the biggest issue I faced while in Portland because of the obvious financial strain I was under. Having to still pay my student loans, buy food, pay rent for the places I was staying at (through Airbnb), and the couple times I would travel to interviews (with Uber or Lyft), made being there quite difficult. Truth be told, I didn’t get to do too much in Portland except for the occasional movie or dinner, but my overall experience was certainly pleasant.

Houston Return

Moving back to Houston (or even Texas for that matter) was, in a way, a unique experience that I wasn’t expecting. I mean, I once lived here before going to Beloit, and before moving to Baltimore, but moving back after being mostly away for the previous 8 years was it’s own form of culture shock. I had lost touch with a lot of friends from high school, and the reality I faced was I didn’t really have any friends there anymore. There were some I wished to remain in contact with, but they felt too distant at this point for me to really feel comfortable with contacting.

For the first time in a long time, I felt a sense of loneliness, despite being surrounded by my family the whole time. I went through some moments of depression, though I mentally remained steadfast knowing any episode of depression was only temporary. After the first 2 weeks of being home passed, I felt myself accepting of my circumstances, and simply moved forward with doing what I could with my life as I had always done. Was it sad to let go of friends I had once been so close with? Yeah, totally, but I still enjoy the memories we had, and I think that’s the important part to me, I feel no animosity toward any of them for what hands life dealt us.

After that, I continued my job search in Houston, or anywhere nearby, really trying to get any job that would accept me (yeah, that’s what I did). I read before that you should only apply to jobs you want to do, but I think anyone who has been unemployed for 7 months reaches a point where being picky begins to be detrimental. The funny thing is that the only thing I look for in a job is if I can work with the people I need to work with. Anything other than that, I truly feel I can learn to do it as required.

This is how I ended up accepting a job in San Antonio, even though my responsibilities would be truly different from what I had done before professionally. I would need to learn all new tech and skills, in a new work environment, in a new city, with all new coworkers. It was slightly intimidating at first, but I’ve always been quick to ask questions and willing to admit when I can be better at something.

Being in San Antonio has been enjoyable so far, and the new job is coming to me slowly, but surely, and eventually I know I’ll succeed at my endeavors in learning all these new things.
