1. Introduction

I sat in front of my computer for the better part of an hour, trying to figure out how to start my introduction, until I decided to just start typing, and see where it goes.

My name is Don Robles, and if you’re here, I imagine you’d like to know more about me. Well, I’m happy to report you’ve come to the right place, after all, this is my website! I usually don’t take many things seriously (mainly myself), except for what needs serious attention (like myself), and I like to think this site will reflect that. Maybe. I’m not sure yet. Part of me wants it to focus solely on my skills, portraying me as a professional with desires to accomplish everything I set my mind to. The other part kinda just wants to finish this thing because it’s been on my to-do list for a more than a few years!

In the end, both those tracks of thought convey who I am to a T (why is that a phrase? “To a T”?) because for better or worse I’m both those people, and without one of them, I wouldn’t be me1. I say that because my two greatest passions, thus far in my short life, are building things, and solving problems. These two joys are at the base of everything I deem worthwhile undertaking.

These joys are my guide in what I do due to the nature of their interaction within myself. I want to solves problems like a coder, breaking them down into bite size pieces, addressing each of them until the full solution is implemented, while my desire to build and create influences how the problem is solved. I’d find myself pulling from my past experiences, building with previous code pieces I created, putting them together into a unique solution that would serve me again in the future.

In layman’s terms, I’m a coder, and an artist. A professional, while being a silly guy, or as I like to put it, I’m a focused individual with a carefree personality. There was a time when I thought I had to drop one part of myself to focus solely on the other, but I’ve since learned they’re intrinsically linked to who I am, and I’m destined to continue the struggle of satiating both sides.

I mean, honestly, it’s not that hard, and I rather enjoy it too as it keeps life fresh for me. One day I’ll be in a bout of solving life’s greatest problems, while another I’m furiously scribbling away in my sketchbook. Maybe one day I’ll truly let one go (I doubt it), but until then, I know I’ll always have something to keep my days occupied!

Thanks for reading, I hope you enjoy what you learn about me! (I promise, I’m cooler in person!)

Don Robles

2. Hobbies/Interests

I have a lot of interests, but really only 2 worth mentioning.

Drawing

My obsession with drawing started out like anything else, a strong desire to get rid of my boredom. The boredom was due to being in a less-than-stimulating class my Freshman year of college. My professor droned on and on about a topic that did nothing for me personally, and in that state, with a need to avoid falling asleep, I began to doodle anything I could see in the class.

It started with an outline of the ceiling projector, catching its curves as best as I can, and it progressed to outlining each connection available behind the projector. From there, I found my way upward, drawing the ceiling mount, and even further from there getting each ceiling tile. I kept at it, making sure to capture the perspective of my view of the projector as best I could, and before I knew it, the class was over.

It was a moment of awakening for me as I thought, “Why not take an art class?” realizing anything that could hold my attention the way drawing did, is at least worth looking into. After that, I took my first art class in the fall semester of my sophomore year, another the following spring, declared my Studio Art major at the end of the year, and have never looked back since.

Music

I don’t remember the exact moment I started to love music, but it’s always been a part of my life. Even though my musical preferences have changed with the years, music itself has held a draw I never get tired of feeling. Whether it was because of its beat, the chorus, or simply being catchy to the ear, my love of music started with simply feeling joy of hearing my (at the time) favorite song.

Over the years, this love matured into understanding the meaning of songs, hearing the lyrics tell a story, teach a lesson, or portray an experience. It was new for me, and it gave me a new reason for keeping my ears tuned into a song, listening to it intently than merely hearing it.

The biggest leap that occurred for me musically wasn’t when I began to think about the construction of a song, but when I began relating to what they were saying. There were songs perfectly explaining what I felt, be it regret, anger, relief, excitement, or ambition to do more. This drove me to a newfound appreciation of songs from my past, finding a greater meaning in some of them as I was now able to truly comprehend what the artist was saying.

Music has been an escape for me throughout my life, providing me whatever I’m looking for, time and time again.

Cloud Computing

My interest in Cloud Computing was sudden, but distinct and immediately enthralling to me. While working on a company project, my role was shifted from project coder, to company DevOpts team member. I was moved because being open to learning new things, and the team lead wanted to bring on a new coder to the team, I volunteered to make space on the team for them (the room was already at capacity).

Joining the DevOpts team started off puzzling, as I had to learn a wide variety of tools in the AWS platform, but with each passing day, I found myself continuously drawn into the world of Cloud Computing. Whereas before I had to learn how to code buttons, levers, and switches, with AWS the level of abstraction stopped at already predefined buttons, levers, and switches.

With that distinction, I learned the possibilities of what could be done with AWS were far smaller than what could be done with code, and it provided me with a base on which I was truly comfortable. Freedom to create something, but without being bogged down in the details of how everything works. Learning AWS has been a joy, and the experience is only getting better as I put more time into grasping everything it can do!

Shell Commands

Now I know what you’re thinking, “Who DOESN’T love for shell commands?!” and I’m right there with you! Shell commands are the greatest. Thing. On. EARTH! They give you immediate feedback, the commands are always the same, and, get this, they’re purely functional! What’s not to like about that? Seriously, I fail to understand how everybody doesn’t love using a Shell in their day-to-day life (convenience be damned).

But seriously, the immediate-response-like nature of a shell gives me a joy I rarely find. If I have an automation problem, need to test some basic functionality, or even if I want to be lazy with the setup of my environment, the shell provides the joy to the solution I implement.

If I could, I’d work in a shell for everything I’d ever need to do! I legitimately mean that. Plus, I feel like you look like a genius when you doing everything from a shell.

Basketball

I love basketball (any James Harden fans?). That’s funny to write because as a kid, I had absolutely no interest in basketball, or even sports. To my younger self, sports weren’t worth watching when I could play video games (that’s all I did until college), but oh how times have changed! (I also watch League of Legends on Twitch)

Basketball is awesome! It’s competitive! It’s constant entertainment! Sports are all about who can physically and mentally perform at the absolute highest levels, against others who are doing the same, and still come out on top!

After watching basketball for a few years now, I’ve found it easier to push myself harder and harder to achieve my desires. Seeing what others have done, sticking to their passions for year after year, grinding day in and day out, putting in hour after hour of work, sweat, blood, and tears, I decided I have no excuse for trying to do the same!

That’s why I love basketball.

Daydreaming

So you know when you’re in a coffee shop, maybe to meet a friend, or maybe to get your morning cup of Joe, and while you wait, you look around and see that guy sitting at a window sill, his fingers interlaced beneath his chin, staring aimlessly out the window? That’s me. And my day isn’t complete without a daydream. (you’ll rarely see me like this)

Since I was a child, daydreaming has been natural and innate to me. I’d always stare out the window on long drives, looking at the clouds, the street lights, the trees, the cows, the grass, or even the other cars. Anything that was on the other side of that window was more interesting to me than what was within 3 feet of me, and it remains the same over 20 years later.

Even now as I type this, I find myself constantly looking at this tree in front of me, the apartment building to my left, or the clouds in the sky. They’re just there, apart of the world, but in the background of our daily lives, and by acknowledging its presence, I’m giving it life and meaning.

Anyway, daydreaming, it’s cool to me. Try it out sometime!

3. Accomplishments

Authentic Athens Marathon

Have you ever ran a marathon? No? Keep it that way, because it was definitely the hardest thing I’ve done in my life, physically and mentally (but really, run one if you want too). The grueling experience taught me just how much effort and determination I could muster to accomplish a goal I set for myself. It required every ounce of my self-discipline to keep my feet moving, step after step, for what felt like an eternity, and what did I get out of it? Almost nothing, except for the brownie points of being able to say “I’ve ran a marathon.” That’s not to downplay my achievement, it’s all I really have to “show” to other people about my experience.

However, that’s not all I gained. What I mostly gained was an understanding of the state of my mental fortitude for persevering when I’m under immense physical and mental stress. It has allowed me to look on my problems since then and say, “This isn’t nearly as exhausting as the marathon was, I can handle this.” And continue operating at a mental level I know I can sustain.

Even now when I work out, I tell myself really, really, REALLY mean things knowing the pain, soreness, exhaustion, and effort is nothing in comparison (it really isn’t), and I continue to push myself harder. I don’t know if I’d ever run another marathon, but it was certainly worth all the effort and training required of me to complete it.

Oh yeah, and in case you’re wondering, I ran the Authentic Marathon in Greece on 11-12-16 with a time of 5:04:17.

A Year of Knod

In 2015, I had a realization of how unhappy I was with my current creative state-of-mind, and so set out to remedy the situation. Prior to starting this project, I attempted to do 200 drawings in a year, but failed because of creative barriers I couldn’t surpass with pure effort (I literally ran out of ideas for drawings). Using that experience, this project aimed to resolve the issues I had with the first project.

At the time, I was really interested in graffiti art, and so I knew I wanted to draw words. From there, I wrote a 52-word poem about the project itself, and the dedication required to complete the whole thing. I chose the poem to be 52 words long because my intention was to do a drawing a week, for a year, so I’d have 52 drawings of words that make up a poem about the dedication required for its completion.

After having the foundation of the project laid down, I officially started the project on April 27th, 2015 and kept up the pace of a drawing a week, for about 4 weeks!! It was lame to fall behind, but my determination never wavered, and after a couple life setbacks, I completed the final drawing on December 31st, 2016. I didn’t plan it to end on the last day of the year, that was a funny coincidence, but when I saw the year coming to a close, and have only 2 drawings left to do, I stopped everything else I was doing to make sure I completed them before the year concluded.

Full Tough Mudder

My decision to do this “tough” event was one of the more spontaneous things I’ve had the joy of doing! It all simply started when my friend on posted a question Facebook asking who wanted to do one? And me never being one to say “No” to life, said “Sure, why not?” and that was the extent of my reasoning for doing it!

You may wonder, what’s a “Tough Mudder”? Well, it’s a 10 mile obstacle course with an obstacle at each half-mile mark, that each basically requires help from someone else to truly complete. That means the whole course is built on needing teamwork to get through it, with myself being on a team of 5 people, 3 of whom I never met before the event itself.

I trained for about 3 months to make sure I’d be in proper condition to complete the full 10 mile course, and I’m certainly glad I did because when the day of the event came, I was slightly surprised at the “ease” of it. That’s not to say I had no help! I certainly did (and it’s required on some obstacles), but the physical toll on my body wasn’t nearly as draining as I was anticipating.

Perhaps it was because I was accustomed to running long distances from my prior marathon? But it didn’t detract from my sense of accomplishment once the event was done. If you decide to do one, I highly recommend having a team of friends do it with you, that’s truly where the joy of the event lies!

Landing an Ollie

Since I was a kid, I’ve always had an admiration for skateboarding. I even had a skateboard, but that was my whole experience. Growing up, I’d watch guys do tricks, envying their skills and how cool they looked. I watched skateboarding videos, shows, and liked skateboarding video games. It never took up a lot of time (and still doesn’t), but it’s always been an interest I kept at arms-length for no real reason. That is, until one day I decided I wanted to land an Ollie.

If you know nothing about skateboarding, allow me to enlighten you. Every skateboarder you see, whether it’s a pro on TV, a guy in the park, or a kid in the driveway, each of them HAD to land an Ollie. It’s honestly a right-of-passage amongst skateboarders because if you tell a skateboarder you’re trying to land an Ollie, they’ll understand exactly what you’re going through. Why did each of them have to land an Ollie? Because it’s literally just jumping with your skateboard.

That makes an Ollie the basis for every modern day trick, just getting the board in the air. It sounds simple enough, but the technique to do so is not natural, intuitive, or even fully understood, and you’re going to fall a lot while learning. It requires a certain amount of finesse and timing that can only be learned with multiple attempts because you’re trying to get the board in the air, maintain control of it, and then land on it, while moving.

As a result, I definitely earned a few scars as a testament to the effort of landing an Ollie, but the day I did, I felt a strong sense of accomplishment for doing what I set my mind to. Since then, I’ve had no desire to go pro (maybe one day), and I still don’t skateboard much, but knowing I did was enough for me to satisfy my skateboarder desires.

1 Hour Drawings

So this project is actually ongoing because I don’t know exactly when it’ll end. Given my current work/life/project balance, I don’t have time for a large artistic endeavor, so this my answer to that. Rather than focus on a large project with interconnected meaning between each drawing like I did with A Year of Knod, the goal here is to simply spend one hour a week actively drawing.

Unlike my 200 Drawings in a Year project, this one is aimed purely at drawing, with no set number of drawings expected for completion. I could choose to focus purely on lines for one drawing, switch to doing something complete freehand, change it up for exploring an idea I had in passing, or even staring at some paper and drawing something abstract. Whatever it is I draw, my focus is purely on drawing for drawing’s sake.

This gives me a format to keep drawing, but with loose enough guidelines that I can explore my creative mindset without the burden of meeting an overall goal.

Getting In Shape

A funny irony with how I completed this was I actually didn’t try to do it (at least effort-wise). There was a time when I liked to eat a lot of food, and while in college, for lunch, I’d always eat two sandwiches with chips, every day. Occasionally I’d have something different, but sandwiches have always been a staple of diet since I was a kid, so it was I never got tired of them. After some time, I had what you could call an epiphany, and it was in the form of noticing I was slightly uncomfortable with eating two sandwiches.

It wasn’t an emotional feeling, but a physical one. My stomach physically felt uncomfortable with two sandwiches inside it, and so I decided to just eat one sandwich for lunch instead of two. Little did I know that this one simple change in my diet would result in me dropping 20 pounds over the course of 9 months. The really funny thing was that I didn’t realize I was losing weight until people started commenting how thin I looked. Shortly after that, my pants began to fit less and less properly, spurning me to continue improving my overall health.

Since then, I’ve continued improving my overall health to a place that I think is truly worth talking about, though I don’t talk about the maintenance of my weight very often.

AWS Certification

Right now I’d say this is my biggest interest, right after drawing. Getting my AWS Certification started off as a simple foray into what AWS could offer my team at the time, and gradually grew into a desire to earn their certification. The main reason I’m so interested in AWS is because of its inherent technical imitations. With coding, you could get endlessly lost in how you want to solve a problem, juggling solutions to try and find the right one. AWS doesn’t have that much complexity.

In AWS, there’s an actual limit to everything you can know about the services because AWS is, itself, a potential solution to business problems. Creativity is required to determine what services you want to use, and how you want to use them, but the underlying tools themselves remain the same. One way I like to explain it is that with coding, you create buttons, levers, and dials, and with AWS you work with buttons, levers, and dials.

Oh yeah, and I just think cloud technology is cool. There’s something intriguing to me about using a random computer node within a giant network to solve my problems. Like the problem of this hosting this website, it’s actually running on AWS right now!

4. History

Houston Start

I was born in Houston, Texas where I lived with my 3 siblings and both parents. I’m the 2nd youngest and needless to say, I was slightly different from everyone else because, as my dad put it, “You were always focused on something. I don’t know what, but you could see it.” (I think I know what he means now). Anyway, I was also the only one who played video games most of the day, and I also enjoyed riding my bike around the park across the street from my house. There was always a curiosity I had for seeing new places or new things, I wanted to know what else the world had.

Sadly, those opportunities were rare in my childhood because of a family burden that couldn’t be avoided. The reason for that was because my father had two jobs, one during most of the day, and the other was throwing newspapers during the early morning hours, but it wasn’t really “his” job, it was the family job.

You see, you don’t get paid by the hour for delivering newspapers, you get paid by how many papers you deliver, and when you have 4 kids who can help you, you can deliver a lot of newspapers! Now that’s not a knock against my parents, it’s just what we needed to do to get through life, and I’m thankful for the experience because it taught me how to work long and hard at something I don’t enjoy, which has served me well, but it wasn’t without it’s drawbacks.

As I mentioned earlier, I could never spend the night at friends houses to play games or do whatever kids do (honest question, what do they do?) And over the 18 years of life before college, we only ever went on 3 vacations that lasted longer than 3 days. I never got to “go out” when I was growing up. I couldn’t hang out with friends for long in the evening at their place. I could stay late at parties. There was no real night life for me as a teenager.

In retrospect, I’m somewhat thankful for avoiding all that because those night activities don’t interest me even now, but part of me feels like I missed out on something that is common for everyone else. Still, I found making friends hard when I was younger because I always felt like there was some kind of constraints on me to figure out who I was, making it hard to connect with others.

Beloit College

Going to Beloit College was by far one of the best things I’ve ever done with my life, right next deciding I was going to be a good person. There was so much that went into my experience of going here, that to write it all down would take building another site with chapters for each year. It was everything I was looking for in a college, and it’ll always have a tender place in my heart for the memories and friends I made there.

The only reason I found out about Beloit College was because my Calculus teacher from high school told me about it, and she only told me about it because she read about it in a book about “Colleges that change your mind about college”. Me, being the unaware high school student I was, thought, “I’ll apply” without much thought afterward, knowing only that it was in Wisconsin and not in Texas. That was really my whole decision-making process for picking a college, that it was outside of Houston (or Texas).

I had little desire to go where I knew my friends were going because I wanted to see more of the world, to go somewhere truly different, and to finally escape needing to deliver newspapers. I only applied to Houston schools to make sure I got in somewhere. After getting accepted, and seeing their generous financial aid package, I immediately chose Beloit College, blindly go somewhere I had never been, and I literally mean that.

I knew nothing about Beloit College other than what I read about it online, their school website, and the acceptance package I received in the mail. I had received an invitation to visit the campus, but was already visiting another college the same weekend as their retreat, so the first time I saw the campus was the day I arrived before orientation. When I later told people about this, they were amazed I could do something like that given I would be there for four years, but it never really fazed me.

As I said, my only concern was the college being outside Houston, other than that, I knew I could work with my situation wherever I went. I still feel that way too, and I think it has served me well thus far!

Baltimore Jump

Before my decision to move to Baltimore, I lived my life like I was on train, sitting down as I moved from event to event with little effort. I was a child, I went to elementary school, then middle school, followed by high school, and rounding it out with college. Going to Baltimore was the first real instance when I truly grasped the understanding of, “I can do anything I want.” and more than that, “I can do it however I wanted.” It was an amazing feeling.

The whole reason I moved there was because my best friend and I made an agreement our senior year at Beloit that wherever he went to graduate school, I’d move there, and if he didn’t get into graduate school, he’d move to Houston with me. He would eventually get accepted at Johns Hopkins and, being true to my word, I moved out there on October 3rd of 2012.

The crazy thing about moving there was my plan (or rather my lack of one) on how I did it. I had no place lined up. I had no job lined up. I barely had money to fund myself while there. The only thing I had was my best friend, which I think is the only reason I was able to pull off such a (in hindsight) reckless move. I was on foodstamps for the first few months because I had no income. I lived off beans and rice because it was cheap. And I took the first job offer I could get, which was thankfully a tech company.

The timing of getting that job couldn’t have been better either because 1 month after I started, my funds were completely dried up, I had less than 100 dollars in my account, and I had to begin paying my student loans (like many others my age). It was definitely a stressful (and fun) time of my life, but it was a truly unique experience to move there with almost nothing, and working my way up to having a place of my own, with a fridge full of food!

Portland Adventure

My need for adventure was 90% of the reason why I decided to move to Portland, OR. As I said before, I moved to Baltimore as part of my agreement with my best friend, but I had no intention of settling there, so I always had a desire to leave at some point. I choose Portland to move to because I was originally going to transfer to the Portland office of the company I was working for. Sadly, that didn’t pan out because I was laid off 2 months before my intended departure, but since I was already getting out my current lease, and looking at places to live there, I figured, “Why not move to Portland?” and so I did!

My original plan wasn’t too much of a plan. I was intending on doing the same thing I had done when I moved to Baltimore, which was to go there with no job or place lined out, and figure it out as I went, with the key difference this time being I didn’t have a friend to stay with while out there. I must admit, this adventure of mine didn’t pan out quite how I would have like. Suffice it to say, this adventure was a failure.

Not having a job lined up was perhaps the biggest issue I faced while in Portland because of the obvious financial strain I was under. Having to still pay my student loans, buy food, pay rent for the places I was staying at (through Airbnb), and the couple times I would travel to interviews (with Uber or Lyft), made being there quite difficult. Truth be told, I didn’t get to do too much in Portland except for the occasional movie or dinner, but my overall experience was certainly pleasant.

Houston Return

Moving back to Houston (or even Texas for that matter) was, in a way, a unique experience that I wasn’t expecting. I mean, I once lived here before going to Beloit, and before moving to Baltimore, but moving back after being mostly away for the previous 8 years was it’s own form of culture shock. I had lost touch with a lot of friends from high school, and the reality I faced was I didn’t really have any friends there anymore. There were some I wished to remain in contact with, but they felt too distant at this point for me to really feel comfortable with contacting.

For the first time in a long time, I felt a sense of loneliness, despite being surrounded by my family the whole time. I went through some moments of depression, though I mentally remained steadfast knowing any episode of depression was only temporary. After the first 2 weeks of being home passed, I felt myself accepting of my circumstances, and simply moved forward with doing what I could with my life as I had always done. Was it sad to let go of friends I had once been so close with? Yeah, totally, but I still enjoy the memories we had, and I think that’s the important part to me, I feel no animosity toward any of them for what hands life dealt us.

After that, I continued my job search in Houston, or anywhere nearby, really trying to get any job that would accept me (yeah, that’s what I did). I read before that you should only apply to jobs you want to do, but I think anyone who has been unemployed for 7 months reaches a point where being picky begins to be detrimental. The funny thing is that the only thing I look for in a job is if I can work with the people I need to work with. Anything other than that, I truly feel I can learn to do it as required.

This is how I ended up accepting a job in San Antonio, even though my responsibilities would be truly different from what I had done before professionally. I would need to learn all new tech and skills, in a new work environment, in a new city, with all new coworkers. It was slightly intimidating at first, but I’ve always been quick to ask questions and willing to admit when I can be better at something.

Being in San Antonio has been enjoyable so far, and the new job is coming to me slowly, but surely, and eventually I know I’ll succeed at my endeavors in learning all these new things.
